Integrating Writing and Design Portfolio

Design Beginnings


Design Fuego

This introductory website offers a glimpse into who I am as a designer and student — my major in Digital Narrative and Interactive Design, and my love of art, books, and travel. Building it challenged me to navigate HTML and CSS hands-on, particularly placing images correctly within the layout.

Logo A/B/C

Three logo variations designed to represent my personal art studio, HoneyDew. Experimenting with color palettes and simplified forms, each iteration explored a different visual direction while maintaining a cohesive identity rooted in my creative brand.

UX Testing Logo Project

Taking the HoneyDew logo designs from the previous project, I conducted UX testing with three participants to gauge which direction resonated most. Their feedback led to meaningful revisions around text readability and color — teaching me how real audiences experience design differently than the designer does.

Cartoon Portrait

A self-portrait created in Adobe InDesign while exploring the program's core tools. Using the paintbrush as my primary tool, I developed a cartoon representation of myself — a playful exercise in translating identity into illustration.

Good Design

A PDF document created to communicate the principles of good design to a general audience. Using color, harmony, and contrast as my guiding principles, I aimed to make the document itself a demonstration of the concepts it explains — letting the design do as much teaching as the words.

Course Goals


1. Consider the role of rhetoric, technology, and ethics in acts of making.

Design Fuego asked me to think carefully about how I present myself to an audience. Every choice — what to include, what image to use, how to arrange content — is a rhetorical act. I had to consider what impression I wanted to make and how the technology either helped or limited that.

2. Leverage Design Principles.

Even in a simple intro page, I applied principles like hierarchy and alignment to make the content readable and visually organized. Working through three logo variations pushed me to apply principles like contrast, simplicity, and color theory deliberately. I learned that small changes in hue or form can dramatically shift how a design feels.

3. Engage with others in a workshop environment.

Seeing how classmates introduced themselves through their sites helped me reflect on my own choices. Getting feedback on three different logo directions was valuable — classmates responded differently to each version, which helped me see how varied audiences read visual choices differently.



4. Practice revision within and across media forms.

My biggest struggle in Design Fuego was getting images to display correctly in the code — working through that required me to revise, test, and revise again. UX testing pushed this further: I took existing logo work, gathered evidence about what wasn't working, and made targeted changes based on real feedback.

5. Learn to present your work.

This portfolio is how I present each project. Framing my work with context helps viewers understand not just what I made, but why and what I learned. The cartoon self-portrait and Good Design document both demonstrate my ability to not just apply design principles but articulate them.

Short Form


Two-page Spread

A reproduced 11x17" two-page magazine spread modeled after an editorial feature on "The Prettiest Boy in the World," created in Adobe InDesign. Rather than filling space freely, I first studied the original's aesthetic — analyzing its layout, typography, and visual rhythm — then worked to faithfully reproduce those elements in my own version.

Infographic

An infographic exploring the science of baking as an applied science — covering ingredients as reactants, mixing techniques, heat transfer, and structural setting. The challenge was balancing rich scientific content with a visually engaging layout that draws the audience in and keeps them reading.

Advertisement

A print advertisement for Ice Breakers Frost mints built around the tagline "Breathe Bold." Using a cool blue palette, bold typography, and confident copywriting, the goal was to create an ad that felt energetic and persuasive — connecting the product to moments of confidence in everyday life.

Accessible Design

A design document exploring what accessibility means in game design — covering visual, motor, cognitive, and auditory accessibility. This project pushed me to think about design as something that should include everyone, and to communicate those principles clearly to an audience who may be new to the concept.

Course Goals


1. Consider the role of rhetoric, technology, and ethics in acts of making.

Each short form project required me to think carefully about audience and purpose. The infographic had to make complex science accessible; the advertisement had to persuade; the accessibility document had to advocate. Every design choice carried rhetorical weight — color, layout, and tone all work together to shape how a message lands.

2. Leverage Design Principles.

Across these projects I applied principles like contrast, hierarchy, and color harmony in different ways depending on the goal. The advertisement used bold type and a cool palette to create energy, while the infographic used warm earth tones and structured sections to guide the reader through dense information.

3. Engage with others in a workshop environment.

Workshop feedback on these shorter pieces helped me understand how quickly first impressions form. Viewers responded to the visual tone before reading a single word, which taught me how much design communicates on its own.



4. Practice revision within and across media forms.

Working across an infographic, advertisement, and accessibility document in the same section pushed me to revise not just individual pieces but my overall approach to layout and communication. Each format has different constraints, and moving between them sharpened my flexibility as a designer.

5. Learn to present your work.

Short form work has to speak quickly and clearly — there is no room to over-explain. Presenting these pieces in my portfolio means trusting the design to communicate first, and using my written descriptions to add the context a viewer can't see on their own.

Long Form


Ten-page Spread

A ten-page magazine spread titled the Digital LookBook — a curated celebration of digital illustration and fashion-forward art. This project built on the two-page spread assignment, pushing my understanding of multi-page layout, typographic consistency, and how to guide a reader through a longer editorial experience.

Client Project

A branded merchandise design for Shared Stories, a bookstore built around the idea of building community through books. The brief called for a baseball cap design that captured the store's identity — the result pairs a bold royal blue with orange typography and a stacked books illustration, creating something wearable that doubles as a brand statement.

UX Design Branding Project

A full UX branding project for Hearth — an original company concept I developed to support independent artists by handling the business side so they can focus on their craft. This project involved brand identity, UX design, and a presentation outlining the company's vision, values, and user experience strategy.

Course Goals


1. Consider the role of rhetoric, technology, and ethics in acts of making.

The long form projects each required me to think about design as communication with real stakes. The Digital LookBook involved curating and framing other artists' work responsibly. The Shared Stories cap had to represent an actual client's identity. Hearth asked me to consider how design can ethically support a vulnerable creative community.

2. Leverage Design Principles.

Across ten pages of the LookBook, I had to maintain visual consistency while keeping each spread fresh. The Shared Stories cap required me to think about how design translates across physical media. For Hearth, I applied UX principles to create a brand experience that feels cohesive and trustworthy from logo to interface.

3. Engage with others in a workshop environment.

The client project in particular pushed me to design for someone else's vision rather than my own. Workshop feedback on these longer pieces helped me understand how to defend design decisions and communicate intent clearly — skills that are essential when working with real clients.



4. Practice revision within and across media forms.

Long form work demands sustained revision. The LookBook went through multiple layout iterations before the pacing felt right. Hearth evolved from a rough concept into a fully developed brand with a UX presentation to back it up. Each project taught me that revision at scale is a different skill than revising a single piece.

5. Learn to present your work.

These three projects represent my most developed work in the portfolio. Presenting them means not just showing the final designs, but articulating the thinking, the client relationship, and the creative vision behind each one — which is exactly what professional design presentation requires.
